# Chapter 03: Scrum Framework

If Agile is the philosophy, **Scrum** is the most widely adopted "implementation" of that philosophy. It is a lightweight framework that helps teams generate value through adaptive solutions for complex problems. Scrum is highly structured, defining specific **Roles**, **Events**, and **Artifacts** to ensure a predictable rhythm of delivery.

---

## 3.1 The Three Roles
Scrum does not recognize traditional corporate hierarchies (like "Senior VP"). Instead, it defines three specific accountabilities:

1.  **The Product Owner (PO):** The "Voice of the Customer." They are responsible for maximizing the value of the product and managing the Product Backlog. They decide *what* gets built and in what order.
2.  **The Scrum Master:** The "Coach." They are responsible for promoting and supporting Scrum. They help the team by removing blockers and facilitating ceremonies. They are a servant-leader, not a "boss."
3.  **The Development Team:** The "Creators." A cross-functional group of professionals (engineers, designers, testers) who do the work of delivering a shippable increment every sprint. They are self-organizing—they decide *how* to do the work.

---

## 3.2 The Five Events (Ceremonies)
Scrum operates in a cycle called a **Sprint** (typically 2 weeks). Within that sprint, four other events occur:

1.  **Sprint Planning:** The team meets to define the **Sprint Goal** and select items from the Product Backlog to include in the **Sprint Backlog**.
2.  **Daily Scrum (Stand-up):** A 15-minute sync for the developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the plan for the next 24 hours.
3.  **Sprint Review (The Demo):** Held at the end of the sprint to inspect the increment and adapt the Product Backlog. This is where you show the working software to stakeholders.
4.  **Sprint Retrospective:** The final event where the team inspects *itself* and creates a plan for improvements to be enacted during the next sprint.



---

## 3.3 The Three Artifacts
Artifacts represent work or value. They are designed to provide transparency:

* **Product Backlog:** An emergent, ordered list of what is needed to improve the product.
* **Sprint Backlog:** The set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint, plus a plan for delivering them.
* **Increment:** The sum of all the Product Backlog items completed during a Sprint, which must meet the **Definition of Done**.

---

## 3.4 The Scrum Values
For Scrum to work, the team must live by five core values:
* **Commitment:** To achieving the goals of the Scrum Team.
* **Focus:** On the work of the Sprint.
* **Openness:** About the work and the challenges.
* **Respect:** For each other's skills and independence.
* **Courage:** To do the right thing and work on tough problems.

---

## 🛠 Hands-on: Identifying Your Role
In your current or next project, observe the "Power Dynamics":
1.  **Is your PO clear on priorities?** If the team is confused about what to work on next, the Product Backlog likely needs refinement.
2.  **Is your Scrum Master protecting the team?** If outside stakeholders are interrupting your sprint with "emergency" requests, the Scrum Master should be the shield.
3.  **Is the Team self-organizing?** During planning, does the manager tell you which tasks to take, or does the team pull tasks based on capacity?
