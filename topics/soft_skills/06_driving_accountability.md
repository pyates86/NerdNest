# Chapter 06: Driving Accountability

Once you have mastered personal ownership, the next challenge is scaling that mindset to the rest of your team. **Driving Accountability** is not about being a "taskmaster" or a "policeman"; it is about creating a social environment where high standards are the norm and everyone feels empowered to hold each other—including leadership—to their word.

---

## 6.1 The "Accountability Gap"
An Accountability Gap occurs when there is a difference between what was expected and what was delivered, but no one addresses it. 
* **The Engineer's Trap:** We often avoid calling out missed commitments because we want to be "nice" or we fear conflict. 
* **The Reality:** By not addressing the gap, you are silently "lowering the bar" for the entire team. This eventually leads to resentment and project delays.

---

## 6.2 Peer-to-Peer Accountability
On the highest-performing teams, accountability doesn't come from the manager (top-down); it comes from peers (side-to-side).
* **The Stand-up Protocol:** If a teammate said they would finish a task yesterday but haven't, a healthy team asks: *"You mentioned that would be done yesterday—what’s blocking you, and how can we get it over the line today?"*
* **The Goal:** You aren't attacking the person; you are protecting the sprint's integrity.

---

## 6.3 The "Supportive Confrontation" Framework
When you need to hold a peer accountable for a missed commitment, use this 3-step logic to keep the conversation productive:

1.  **State the Observation (The Data):** *"We agreed on the design doc being ready Tuesday, and it’s now Thursday."*
2.  **Ask for the Context (The "Why"):** *"I noticed it hasn't been updated. Did the priorities shift, or did you hit a technical blocker I'm not aware of?"*
3.  **Restate the Impact (The "So What"):** *"Because it’s not ready, the frontend team is currently guessing at the API structure. What do we need to do to get this resolved?"*

---

## 6.4 Leading by Intent (The "Commander's Intent")
To drive accountability, you must first provide **Clarity**. People cannot be held accountable for vague goals.
* **Bad Goal:** "Make the app faster."
* **Accountable Goal:** "Reduce the homepage Load Time (LCP) from 2.5s to 1.2s by the end of Sprint 14."

When you give a teammate a task, ensure they have the **three C's**:
* **Capability:** Do they have the tools/skills to do it?
* **Capacity:** Do they have the time to do it?
* **Commitment:** Have they explicitly said "Yes, I will do this"?

---

## 🛠 Hands-on: The "Commitment Check"
Next time you are in a meeting where a task is assigned:
1.  **Wait for the "Vague Yes":** Listen for phrases like "I'll try to get to that" or "I'll look into it."
2.  **Interject for Accountability:** Say, *"Just so we’re all clear, are you committing to having that finished by [Date], or is that just a 'maybe'?"*
3.  **Observe the Shift:** Notice how the energy changes when a "maybe" is forced into a "yes" or "no." This simple question eliminates 80% of future accountability gaps.
